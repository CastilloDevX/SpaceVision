Composition & structure

Mars is a rocky planet with an iron-oxide–rich crust —hence its red color—, a rocky mantle, and a metallic core (likely partially liquid).

Crust: thin relative to the mantle, blanketed with dust and oxides (hematite).
Mantle: rocky material similar to other terrestrial planets.
Volcanoes: home to Olympus Mons, the largest volcano in the Solar System.
Type: Rocky (terrestrial) planet
Color: red (iron oxides)
Olympus Mons: largest volcano in the system
Diameter: ~6,779 km
Quick summary

Oxidized rocky world; giant volcanoes and geology pointing to ancient activity.

Atmosphere

Mars has a very thin atmosphere composed almost entirely of carbon dioxide (CO₂). Lacking density to retain heat like Earth, its climate is cold and dry.

Fine dust in suspension can trigger planet-wide storms; surface pressure is only a small fraction of Earth’s.
Composition: mainly CO₂
Pressure: very low (~0.6% of Earth)
Climate: cold & arid
Phenomena: dust storms
Fact

The thin air allows large thermal swings and dust storms that can engulf the whole planet.

Motion & orbit

Mars takes about 687 Earth days to orbit the Sun. Its rotation is similar to Earth’s: a day lasts around 24.6 hours, giving comparable day–night cycles.

With an axial tilt, Mars has seasons too — longer than Earth’s because of its longer year.
Year: ~687 Earth days
Rotation: ~24.6 hours
Seasons: present (longer)
Eccentricity: more elliptical than Earth
Motion

Longer year → longer seasons; day length close to Earth’s.

Curiosities & influence

Mars has two small moons —Phobos and Deimos— and shows evidence that liquid water once flowed at the surface. It’s the most explored planet after Earth.

Highlights include ancient riverbeds, hydrated minerals, and polar regions with ice; it hosts giant volcanoes and deep canyons.
Moons: Phobos & Deimos
Water: past water evidence & polar ice
Exploration: many missions in orbit & on the ground
Color: iron oxides (red)
Perspective

Key to understanding planetary evolution and future resource prospects (water/ice) for human missions.

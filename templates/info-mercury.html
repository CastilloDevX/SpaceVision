Composition & structure

Mercury is a rocky world with an oversized metallic core —close to 85% of its radius. Its crust is thin and heavily cratered.

Core: iron–nickel, unusually large relative to the planet.
Crust: thin, densely cratered.
Surface: basaltic plains, scarps, and impact deposits.
Type: Terrestrial (rocky) planet
Core size: ~85% of radius
Diameter: ~4,880 km
Surface: craters & lobate scarps
Quick summary

Huge core, thin crust, impact-scarred face.

Exosphere

Mercury has an extremely tenuous atmosphere —an exosphere— made of oxygen, sodium, hydrogen, helium, and potassium. It cannot trap heat effectively.

Micrometeoroid impacts and surface release replenish the exosphere; surface temperatures swing dramatically between day and night.
Composition: O₂, Na, H, He, K
Heat retention: very low
Day: up to 430 °C
Night: ~ −180 °C
Fact

Exosphere ≠ insulating blanket; Mercury endures extreme thermal swings.

Motion & orbit

Mercury has the fastest orbit in the Solar System — just 88 Earth days per year. Its rotation is slow; a solar day lasts about 176 Earth days.

The 3:2 spin–orbit resonance makes its daytime linger and nights long; a hallmark of its dynamics.
Orbital period: ~88 days
Solar day: ~176 days
Spin–orbit: 3:2 resonance
Mean distance: ~0.39 AU
Motion

Short year, long day; a striking 3:2 resonance.

Curiosities & influence

Temperature extremes range from −180 °C at night to 430 °C by day. It has no moons or rings, and preserves early Solar System history on its surface.

Shadowed polar craters can harbor water ice; global scarps reveal planetary cooling and contraction over time.
Temperature: −180 °C to 430 °C
Moons: none
Rings: none
Why it matters: clues to early Solar System
Perspective

A laboratory for extremes — and for the Solar System’s origin story.

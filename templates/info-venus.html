Composition & Structure

Venus is a rocky planet similar in size to Earth. Its interior includes a metallic core, a rocky mantle, and a surface shaped by widespread volcanism and vast basaltic plains.

Core: mostly iron and nickel.
Mantle: dense, rocky layer.
Surface: volcanoes, lava flows, and broad plains.
Type: Terrestrial (rocky) planet
Size: Earth-sized
Surface: active volcanism, plains
Core: metallic (Fe, Ni)
Quick summary

Earth-like size and structure, but a surface dominated by volcanism and extensive plains.

Atmosphere

Venus’ atmosphere is mostly carbon dioxide (CO₂), with thick clouds of sulfuric acid. This produces the strongest greenhouse effect in the Solar System.

A very dense atmosphere and highly reflective cloud deck trap heat, leading to extreme surface temperatures and surface pressures far above Earth’s.
Composition: CO₂-dominated
Clouds: sulfuric acid
Greenhouse: strongest in the Solar System
Pressure: ~92 atm at surface
Fact

The dense atmosphere yields ~92× Earth’s surface pressure and ~465 °C temperatures.

Motion & Orbit

Venus takes 225 Earth days to orbit the Sun. Its rotation is retrograde and extremely slow: a sidereal day lasts about 243 Earth days, meaning it spins opposite most planets.

Because of the slow, retrograde rotation, a solar day on Venus is longer than its year. This affects wind patterns and atmospheric circulation.
Orbital period: ~225 days
Rotation: retrograde, ~243 days
Day vs year: day longer than year
Mean distance: ~0.72 AU
Motion

The retrograde, sluggish spin drives unique atmospheric dynamics compared to Earth.

Curiosities & Influence

Venus is the hottest planet, with surface temperatures around ~465 °C. From Earth it appears as the bright morning or evening star.

Its dense atmosphere, volcanism, and high albedo make it a prime target to study extreme climates and planetary evolution.
Temperature: ~465 °C (stable)
Visibility: very bright in Earth’s sky
Activity: volcanism (possible current activity)
Interest: greenhouse limits & atmospheric evolution
Perspective

Studying Venus helps define the extremes of greenhouse physics and atmospheric evolution.

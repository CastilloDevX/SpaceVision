Composition & structure

The Sun is a G2V-type star composed mainly of hydrogen (~74%) and helium (~24%). Its interior is stratified into the core, radiative zone, and convective zone; its atmosphere includes the photosphere, chromosphere, and corona.

Core: where nuclear fusion occurs.
Radiative zone: energy travels outward by radiation over thousands of years.
Convective zone: energy is transported by convection up to the photosphere.
Type: G2V
H/He: ~74% / 24%
Core temperature: ~15 million °C
Diameter: ~1.39×106 km
Quick summary

Layered structure — each layer plays a key role in how energy is generated and transported.

Energy source

Nuclear fusion in the core converts hydrogen nuclei into helium — the proton–proton chain — releasing energy that travels to the surface as photons and heat.

Each reaction releases particles and photons; neutrinos escape almost immediately — detecting them confirms the core reactions.
Power: ~3.8×1026 W
Chain: proton–proton
Neutrinos: detectable on Earth
Impact: sustains life (photosynthesis)
Fact

Energy produced in seconds in the core takes thousands of years to reach the surface as light.

Influence on Earth

Solar radiation drives climate, day/night, and the seasons; magnetic activity produces solar storms that affect satellites, communications, and generate auroras.

Coronal mass ejections (CMEs) and energetic particles interact with the magnetosphere, creating spectacular phenomena and technological risks.
Auroras: particle collisions with the atmosphere
Satellites: communications & atmospheric drag
Power grids: geomagnetically induced currents
Climate: the Sun as energy driver
Caution

Space agencies monitor space weather to protect critical infrastructure.

Solar cycle & future

The Sun has ~11-year activity cycles (spots, flares). We are currently in Solar Cycle 25. In about ~5 billion years it will become a red giant and eventually a white dwarf.

The cycle affects the number of sunspots and the frequency of energetic events; its stellar fate is typical for stars of similar mass.
Cycle: ~11 years
Age: ~4.6 billion years
Future phases: red giant → white dwarf
Timescale: billions of years
Perspective

The processes span enormous time and energy scales — yet are highly predictable at large scales.
